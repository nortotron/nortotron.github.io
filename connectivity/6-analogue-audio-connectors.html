6 - Analogue Audio Connectors

Connector Purpose History Variants
¼-Inch (6.35mm) Jack Used for musical instruments, headphones, and professional audio. Developed in the early 20th century for telephone switchboards, later adapted for audio. TS (Mono unbalanced), TRS (Stereo or balanced mono), TRRS (Stereo + mic).
⅛-Inch (3.5mm) Mini Jack Used in modular synthesis, sequencers, headphones, mobiles, and laptops. Miniaturised from the ¼-inch jack, popularised by Sony Walkmans and MP3 players. TS (Mono), TRS (Stereo), TRRS (Stereo + mic).
RCA (Phono) Used for consumer hi-fi systems, turntables, and older TVs. Developed by RCA in the 1930s for phonographs. Mono (Single plug per channel), Stereo (Red/White pair).
XLR Used in professional audio, microphones, PA systems, and balanced audio. Developed by Cannon Electric in the 1950s. 3-pin (Balanced audio), 4-pin & 5-pin (Intercoms, lighting, stereo mics).
SpeakON Secure speaker connections in live sound and professional audio. Designed by Neutrik in the 1980s for high-power speakers. 2-pole, 4-pole, and 8-pole configurations.
Banana Plug Used in high-end home audio systems, amplifiers, some modular synthesisers and test equipment. Originated in early electrical testing, later adopted for audio. Single and dual banana plugs.
DIN and Mini-DIN Used in older hi-fi systems, MIDI interfaces, and European audio equipment. Developed by the German Institute for Standardization (DIN) in the 1950s. 5-pin DIN (MIDI), Other configurations (Hi-fi, preamps, vintage gear).
